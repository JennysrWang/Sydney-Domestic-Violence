Home "Sweet" Home

The pandemic has discouraged DV victims to report the abuse since they are trapped inside households.

In 2017, there were 29,062 domestic violence cases reported. The month of August had the lowest number with 1,861 cases reported, while the December had the highest with 3,291 cases reported.

There was an increase of 505 domestic violence cases reported from the previous year in 2018, making it 29,567 in total. In May, there were 1,994 cases reported, making it the month with the lowest cases, while the month of December had 3,554 cases reported, making it the month with the highest cases again.

The increase of reported cases has been doubled in 2019. The total cases reported is 31,315 with an increase of 1,648 cases. The month of December has been consistent for having the highest cases reported with 3,466 cases, while the month of May had reported the lowest number with 2,079 cases for this year.

From the repoart of NCOSS in 2020, in NSW there are approximately 2,500 reports of domestic violence to the police every month, but this likely represents only 40% of actual incidents due to underreporting.

Design Rationale

Overview of domestic violence in NSW
Domestic violence incidents are prevalent in Australia, and the number of cases reported in NSW has increasingly doubled in the past decade, which might directly affect the country's collective efforts of the law enforcement and justice system. This will be the primary focus of our report which will compare DV cases across NSW before and after the pandemic as well as to raise awareness that these cases can occur all the time to anyone. We found out that there is an interesting trend happening on a yearly cycle. The reported cases appear high from December to January and gradually slow down from May to July. However, this changed when the COVID-19 pandemic came, and people were stuck at home in lockdown in 2020.

Major visual elements - windows and victims
A couple of iterations and back-up research have been implemented to deliver a more convincing and credible story alongside the data. We decided to focus on the trend changes by storytelling with victims behind windows and blood. First, we used the window as a metaphor for a typical household, and each window represents a particular year. A fully open window symbolises pre-pandemic times, which gradually closes throughout the years as the pandemic comes. On the other hand, a fully closed window implies the start of the pandemic in 2020. Furthermore, sketches of people of different gender and ages are seen behind these windows to show that everyone can be a domestic violence victim.

Data presentation
We applied a bar chart because the long rectangular shape is similar to blood dripping compared to other graphs. We also aim to create a visually appealing image when readers can easily connect the bar chart to blood. We turned the bar graph upside down and coloured it dark red to show a dripping blood effect from the window, depicting the unseen domestic abuse happening behind closed doors. In addition, we put the bars side by side, and the round corners highlight the blood effect further. The X-axis is the weekly average of every month. The Y-axis, which indicates the number of cases per month, is placed on the left side, acting like a ruler for better and easier understanding. A hover effect is added to encourage the audience to interact with the graph more. Lastly, we followed a newspaper layout to show the credibility of the numbers and the people behind reporting these cases.
